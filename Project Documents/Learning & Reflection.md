## 📝 Project Reflection

This project served as an intensive exploration into the physical and technical constraints of embedded systems. Moving from a conceptual schematic to a handheld, 17cm prototype required navigating a series of cascading engineering challenges. The core of the difficulty lay in the spatial reconciliation between the electronic architecture and the mechanical housing.

The initial planning phase was rigorous, involving detailed CAD modeling in Fusion 360 to ensure the ESP32, OLED, and battery pack would physically coexist within the shell. However, the transition to physical assembly revealed the true complexity of the design. The system relied on an overwhelming number of discrete connections, including I2C lines for the HUD, signal lines for the laser and IR emitter, and the high-speed sampling wire for the raw IR receiver. Each component required precise routing through the narrow internal chassis.

Minor issues with soldering joints and wire fatigue necessitated constant continuity checking, as even a single loose ground would compromise the NEC protocol’s timing. Despite exhaustive planning to join the internal mounting plates with the outer shell, the sheer volume of 22AWG wiring eventually exceeded the internal volume of the 3D-printed gun casing. This density of connections ultimately led to the failure of integrating the circuit inside the gun shell. This struggle highlighted a vital lesson in product design: the "logical" space on a circuit board and the "physical" volume of a housing are two vastly different realities that demand significant margin for error.
